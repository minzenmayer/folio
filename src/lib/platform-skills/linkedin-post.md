# LinkedIn post — writing strategy

## Shape

A LinkedIn post that earns the click is built like a TV cold-open. First two lines must do all the work, because they're the only thing the reader sees in feed. The "...see more" cuts after roughly 210 characters on desktop, fewer on mobile.

## Hook patterns that land

Five shapes consistently outperform on LinkedIn for the kind of writing Payton does:

1. **Mundane Moment + Revelation** — "I spent 15 years writing sermons before I ever wrote a subject line. Turns out, they're the same job." Personal-specific opening, then a contrast that reframes.
2. **Bold Claim + Proof Tease** — "Most founders' homepages are killing their conversion. Here's what's actually working." Claim that creates friction, then promise of evidence.
3. **Question That Names a Tension** — "What if everything you know about marketing is wrong?" Works only when the post actually delivers the answer; cheap-shot questions tank.
4. **Specific Number + Counter-Intuitive Insight** — "I A/B tested 47 subject lines. The 3-word ones won." Quantification grants credibility; counter-intuitive insight earns the read.
5. **Story Setup + Stakes** — "Last week I almost missed my flight because I was crying in an airport bookstore. Here's what I learned about B2B copy." Personal stakes, then a turn into the post's actual subject.

## Length

180-240 words is the sweet spot. Shorter than 100 reads as thin; longer than 300 starts losing readers around line 8. Aim for ~180 unless the post genuinely needs the room.

## Line break rhythm

Single sentences. Each line break creates a beat. White space carries the eye. Reader on mobile sees one sentence at a time as they scroll; rhythm matters more than punctuation.

## What to avoid

- Three-item lists at the start ("X, Y, and Z"). Reads as AI.
- "Here's the thing" / "Hot take" / "Unpopular opinion" — burned phrases.
- Vague second-person ("As leaders, we…"). Specific or first-person only.
- Em dashes used as "but actually" pivots. Comma + period is stronger.
- Closing question that asks the reader to comment. The post should earn the comment, not beg for it.

## Closer patterns

- **Through-line restatement** — one sentence that lands the post's argument with no new information.
- **Sharp single sentence** — sometimes a 7-word period is the whole closer.
- **Concrete next move** — "Try this on your next post" works only when the post taught something specific.

## Voice notes for Payton (Christian Story Lab)

The audience is Christian creators / writers / founders. Story-shaped framing lands. Sermon-craft references resonate (most readers came up through some version of pulpit or stage). Avoid "ministry" as the word that frames the work; "story" / "craft" / "stretch" carry more weight without flattening the audience to one tribe.
