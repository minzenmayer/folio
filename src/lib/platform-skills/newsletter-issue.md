# Newsletter issue — writing strategy

## Shape

Newsletter issues for Christian Story Lab follow a Lede + 3-5 Sections + Closer arc. Target ~1000 words. The lede is its own paragraph — sometimes a single sentence — that hooks the reader and frames the question the issue answers.

## Lede patterns

The lede is the most important paragraph. Three patterns consistently land:

1. **Specific scene + named tension** — "I was on the phone with Arlo when Jalen Williams walked out at MSG." Concrete moment that the rest of the issue interrogates.
2. **Question reframed** — "Most writers think the hard part is finding ideas. The hard part is staying with them." Sets up the reframe the issue then earns.
3. **Counter-narrative** — "Everyone's writing about X. Here's what they're missing." Works when the issue actually delivers the missing thing.

## Section discipline

Each section earns its existence by carrying ONE point. Three to five sections, each ~150-250 words. Sections without a clear point become filler and the reader can feel it.

H2 per section. The H2 is not a clever line; it's a clear claim. ("Sermon-craft is the same as subject-line craft" is a section header. "The Hidden Truth Most Writers Miss" is not.)

## Voice cadence

- Sentences vary in length. A long observation followed by a short period creates beat. Three medium sentences in a row sound robotic.
- Specifics over abstractions. "47 subject lines" beats "many subject lines."
- Personal stakes named, not assumed. The reader didn't have your week.
- Active voice. "The team built X" beats "X was built by the team."

## Story-frame strategies

Christian Story Lab's wedge is treating story-craft seriously. That means:

- Open in scene where possible.
- Name the tension before the resolution.
- Withhold the answer until the reader has felt the question.
- Closers should land the through-line in a sentence the reader will remember tomorrow.

## What to avoid

- Em dashes used as "but actually" pivots. They flag AI writing in this audience.
- "It's not just X — it's Y" antithesis flips. Cut.
- Three-item lists of abstract qualities ("bold, faithful, urgent"). Cut.
- Vague second-person plural ("As we navigate the modern landscape…"). Replace with first-person specific.
- "Crucial," "vital," "pivotal," "delve," "tapestry," "underscore." All burned.
- Closing with "What do you think? Reply and let me know." Reader replies because the issue earned a reply, not because it asked.

## Closer patterns

- **Specific next step** — one concrete thing the reader can try this week.
- **Through-line restatement** — the issue's argument in a sentence.
- **Question the reader has to sit with** — only if the issue genuinely earns the question.
- **Personal commitment** — what the writer is doing about the thing they just argued. Risky but lands when sincere.

## Length notes

900-1100 words is the target band. Below 800 and the issue feels thin; above 1300 and engagement drops. CSL's best-performing issues sit around 1050.
